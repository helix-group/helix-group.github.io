My group is interested in understanding drug response at all scales: molecular, cellular, organism/patient and population. We use informatics methods to analyze and integrate data across scales, with the goal of optimizing use of existing drugs and discovering new uses for drugs, alone and in combination. Key application goals include:

  • Using molecular level information of drug targets (3D protein structures) and small molecular modulators/chemicals to predict their interaction and downstream effects. We are particularly interested in linking molecular properties to clinical responses.
  • Using cellular level information (expression, genomics, emerging â€˜omics) to understand systems pharmacology responses to drugs, with the goal of individualizing therapy and finding new uses for drugs alone and in combination. We also build the premier repository of knowledge about the genetic influence on drug response, www.pharmgkb.org/
  • Using patient level information (from EHR, claims data) to understand the un-recognized effects (both good and bad) of drugs alone and in combination, with a particular interest in connecting this to molecular and cellular responses.
  • Regulatory science. We work to define research questions that will help regulatory agencies (e.g. FDA) make better/faster decisions about new therapeutics and diagnostics, focusing on data science, with applications in genomics, mobile health and other areas of emerging focus.

We approach these applications with particular interest in the following methodological approaches:

  • Machine learning (Bayesian, Random Forests, Deep Networks) particularly at the molecular level, taking advantage of growing data sets, but interested at all levels.
  • Natural Language Processing. We develop new methods for extracting semantics in unsupervised ways, and separately use DeepDive system (Chris Re) for large scale extraction of the interactions between genes/mutations, drugs and diseases.
  • Network/graph analysis. We are interested in methods for detecting interesting signals in biological networks that respond to drugs. This includes the use of matrix decomposition methods (e.g. Independent Component Analysis) to define modules that work together during drug response.
  • Chemoinformatics. We are interested in developing methods to summarize the key characteristics/features of small molecules in order to correlate and associate them with molecular, cellular, and organism response.

In addition to NIH support for our work, we are supported by FDA, Pfizer, Genentech.
